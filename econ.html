Time Value Logic

I have to start with the Equivalence Principle: different sums of money at different times can be economically equal. I use Cash Flow Diagrams (CFD) to map these. Arrows up = Inflow (Salvage, Revenue). Arrows down = Outflow (Initial Cost, O&M).

The Compounding Effect

In this course, I never use simple interest. Everything is compound. If a period isn't specified, I assume annual.

Future Value (F): F = P(1 + i)^n
Present Value (P): P = F(1 + i)^-n

Interest Formulas

I use these factors to move money across the CFD. I need to be careful with 'n'—it's the number of compounding periods, not necessarily years.

Uniform Series (Annuities)

When I have a series of equal payments A over n periods:

(P/A, i, n) = [(1+i)^n - 1] / [i(1+i)^n]
(A/P, i, n) = [i(1+i)^n] / [(1+i)^n - 1]

Effective Interest

If interest is "12% compounded monthly," the nominal rate r is 12%, but the effective rate ia is higher because of compounding.

ia = (1 + r/m)^m - 1

Gradient Series

Most costs in engineering increase as equipment ages. I categorize these into Arithmetic and Geometric.

Arithmetic Gradient (G)

The cash flow increases by a constant dollar amount each year (e.g., $100, $200, $300...). The total Present Worth is the base annuity P_A plus the gradient P_G.

Geometric Gradient (g)

The flow grows by a constant percentage (e.g., 5% more each year). I have to check if the interest rate i equals the growth rate g.

If i != g: P = A1[1 - (1+g)^n(1+i)^-n] / (i - g)

Present Worth (PW)

I use PW to discount everything back to t=0. If I'm comparing projects with unequal lives, I must use the Least Common Multiple (LCM) of their lives to create a fair comparison period.

Decision Rule: In a mutually exclusive set, I pick the project with the highest PW (provided PW >= 0).

Annual Worth (AW)

This is usually my go-to for comparing projects with different lifespans because I don't need the LCM. AW assumes the project repeats indefinitely.

AW = (Revenues - Expenses) - Capital Recovery (CR)

Capital Recovery (CR) accounts for the loss in value of the asset over time plus interest on the tied-up capital.

IRR & Incremental Analysis

Internal Rate of Return is the rate where PW = 0. I cannot just pick the highest IRR; that's a common trap. I have to perform Incremental Analysis.

The Steps

1. Rank projects by initial investment.
2. Calculate the difference in cash flows (Δ Cash Flow).
3. Solve for ΔIRR. If ΔIRR > MARR, I choose the more expensive project.

CCA & Depreciation

Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) is the tax-allowable depreciation in Canada. It reduces my taxable income, which creates a "Tax Shield."

The Half-Year Rule

In the first year of ownership, I can only claim 50% of the asset's cost for CCA. I need to remember to subtract the CCA from the UCC (Undepreciated Capital Cost) to find the base for the next year.

After-Tax Flows

I have to account for income tax to find the true viability of a project. BTCF is Before-Tax Cash Flow; ATCF is After-Tax.

Taxable Income = BTCF - CCA
Income Tax = Taxable Income * (Tax Rate)
ATCF = BTCF - Income Tax

Replacement Logic

I compare my current asset (Defender) to a potential new one (Challenger). I ignore "sunk costs" (what I originally paid for the Defender).

Economic Service Life (ESL)

The ESL is the number of years that minimizes the total AW of costs. I replace the Defender if its Marginal Cost for next year is higher than the minimum AW of the Challenger.

Inflation Models

I have to handle two types of dollars: Actual (inflated) and Constant (purchasing power). I use the Market Interest Rate (i) for Actual dollars and the Real Interest Rate (i') for Constant dollars.

(1 + i) = (1 + i')(1 + f)
where f = inflation rate.
