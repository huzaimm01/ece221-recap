Time Value of Money

This is the foundation. Money today is worth more than money tomorrow. I need to remember that all economic comparisons are invalid unless the cash flows are moved to the same point in time (usually t=0 or t=n).

Cash Flow Diagrams (CFD)

I use CFDs to map out everything. Up arrows are positive (Revenue, Salvage Value). Down arrows are negative (Initial Cost, Maintenance, Taxes).

Fundamental Variables

P: Present amount at time zero.
F: Future amount at year n.
A: Uniform series of payments (Annuity).
i: Interest rate per period.
n: Total number of compounding periods.

Interest Formulas

Standard compound interest is the default for this course. Simple interest is never used here.

Single Payment

F = P(1 + i)^n
P = F(1 + i)^-n

Effective Interest Rate

If interest compounds more than once a year, I have to calculate the Effective Annual Rate (ia) to compare it fairly.

ia = (1 + r / m)^m - 1

Where r is the nominal rate and m is the compounding frequency per year.

Gradients & Series

Most real-world maintenance costs aren't flat; they increase every year as equipment ages.

Arithmetic Gradient (G)

This is when the cash flow increases by a constant dollar amount each period.

P = G/i * [((1+i)^n - 1)/i - n] * (1+i)^-n

Geometric Gradient (g)

This is when the flow increases by a fixed percentage (e.g., 5% growth). If i = g, the formula simplifies significantly.

If i != g: P = A1 * [1 - (1+g)^n * (1+i)^-n] / (i - g)
If i == g: P = A1 * n / (1 + i)

Present Worth Analysis

The Present Worth (PW) method collapses every future cost and revenue into one single dollar value at the current moment.

Decision Rules

1. If PW > 0, the project is acceptable.
2. If comparing multiple projects, I pick the one with the highest PW.

Unequal Lives & LCM

If Project A lasts 2 years and Project B lasts 3 years, I can't compare their PW directly. I must use a 6-year study period (Least Common Multiple) and assume Project A is bought 3 times and Project B is bought twice.

Annual Worth Analysis

AW is often better because I don't need to do the LCM thing for unequal lives. It assumes the project repeats indefinitely at the same costs.

Capital Recovery (CR)

This is the annual cost of owning the asset, accounting for the initial price minus the salvage value.

CR = P(A/P, i, n) - S(A/F, i, n)

IRR & Incremental Analysis

Internal Rate of Return is the "break-even" interest rate. I set PW = 0 and solve for i.

The Incremental Rule

When comparing two projects (X and Y), I don't just pick the one with the higher IRR. I have to find the IRR of the difference (Y - X). If that incremental IRR is greater than my MARR, I choose the more expensive project.

CCA & Tax Shield

Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) is how I account for the tax savings from depreciation in Canada.

Half-Year Rule

In the first year I buy an asset, I can only claim half of the depreciation for tax purposes. This is a critical step in GENTECH 2EE3 problems.

Year 1 CCA = (Purchase Price * 0.5) * d
Remaining UCC = (Purchase Price - Year 1 CCA)

After-Tax Cash Flow

I need to remember that taxes are real disbursements. I calculate taxable income first, then the tax, then subtract that from the cash flow.

Taxable Income = Revenue - Expenses - CCA
Income Tax = Taxable Income * Tax Rate
ATCF = (Revenue - Expenses) - Income Tax

Replacement Analysis

This is for deciding whether to keep the "Defender" or buy the "Challenger."

The Rule of Thumb

I replace the Defender if its Marginal Cost for the very next year is higher than the Minimum Annual Worth of the Challenger.

Inflation Models

I have to distinguish between "Actual Dollars" (inflated) and "Constant Dollars" (purchasing power).

Real Rate (i') = (1 + i) / (1 + f) - 1

Where i is the market rate and f is the inflation rate.
