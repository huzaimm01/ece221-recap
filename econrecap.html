Foundations & CFD

I need to remember that engineering economics isn't just about math; it's about decision-making. I have to map every problem onto a Cash Flow Diagram (CFD) first. Upward arrows are positive receipts; downward arrows are disbursements.

The Equivalence Concept

Money has different values at different times. $100 today is not equal to $100 in 5 years. I use the interest rate (i) to move money through time. This is the 'Time Value of Money' (TVM).

If I can't draw the CFD, I can't solve the problem. Always label n, i, P, and F.

Interest & Equivalence

This is where I move P to F or F to P. I'm strictly using compound interest here. Nominal rates are 'r', but I need effective rates 'ia' if compounding is more than annual.

Single Payment (F/P): F = P(1 + i)^n
Present Value (P/F): P = F(1 + i)^-n
Annuity (P/A): P = A * [((1+i)^n - 1) / (i(1+i)^n)]

Effective vs Nominal

If interest is 12% compounded monthly, r = 0.12 and m = 12. The periodic rate is 1%. The effective annual rate is higher.

ia = (1 + r/m)^m - 1

Gradient Series

I have two types of gradients for costs that change over time: Arithmetic and Geometric.

Arithmetic Gradient (G)

This is a linear increase. Year 1: $100, Year 2: $150, Year 3: $200. Here, G = $50. The total Present Worth is the base annuity (the $100) PLUS the gradient component.

Geometric Gradient (g)

This is a percentage increase (e.g., maintenance grows by 5% every year). I have to be careful if i = g because the formula changes.

If i != g: P = A1 * [1 - (1+g)^n * (1+i)^-n] / (i - g)

Comparison Methods

I use Present Worth (PW) and Annual Worth (AW) to decide between projects.

The PW Rule

If I have two projects with different lives (e.g., 3 years and 4 years), I cannot compare them directly via PW. I must use the Least Common Multiple (LCM), which in this case is 12 years. I assume both projects are repeated until they hit 12 years.

The AW Rule

AW is easier for unequal lives. I don't need the LCM. I just find the AW for one life cycle, and that value remains the same regardless of how many times the project is repeated.

IRR & Incremental Analysis

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is the 'i' that makes the PW = 0. I cannot simply pick the project with the highest IRR. That's a trap.

The Incremental Method

1. Rank projects by initial cost.
2. Look at the difference (Project B - Project A).
3. Find the ΔIRR of that difference.
4. If ΔIRR > MARR, I pick the more expensive one (B). If not, I stick with A.

CCA & Depreciation

This is Canada-specific tax logic. I use the Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) to calculate the 'Tax Shield'.

The Half-Year Rule

In the first year I buy something, I can only claim 50% of the cost for depreciation. This is a massive point-sink in the midterm if I forget it.

Year 1 CCA = (Cost * 0.5) * d
Year 2 CCA = (Cost - Year 1 CCA) * d

Replacement Analysis

Defender (what I have) vs. Challenger (what I want). I ignore sunk costs (what I paid for the Defender years ago). I only care about what it's worth now (Salvage Value).

Marginal Cost Logic

I keep the Defender as long as its Marginal Cost for next year is less than the Minimum Annual Worth (Min AW) of the Challenger. Once the Defender's cost for one more year exceeds the Challenger's best average cost, I swap them.

Inflation Models

I have Actual Dollars (what the bank sees) and Constant Dollars (what the money can actually buy). I need to map the interest rate to the dollar type.

Actual Dollars -> Market Interest Rate (i)
Constant Dollars -> Real Interest Rate (i')

Formula: (1 + i) = (1 + i')(1 + f)
