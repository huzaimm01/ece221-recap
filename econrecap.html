Course Overview

GENTECH 2EE3 focuses on the systematic evaluation of the costs and benefits of technical projects. In the engineering world, a technically sound solution is useless if it is not economically viable.

Structure of Learning

The course transitions from simple interest calculations to complex multi-asset replacement decisions. Success depends on understanding the Equivalence Principle—that different sums of money at different times can be equal in value.

Time Value of Money

The core axiom: "A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow." This exists because of inflation, risk, and—most importantly—opportunity cost.

Cash Flow Diagrams (CFD)

Before solving any problem, you must visualize it. Upward arrows represent Receipts (Inflow); downward arrows represent Disbursements (Outflow).

The Five Variables

  • P (Present Value): A sum of money at time zero.
  • F (Future Value): A sum of money at some future time n.
  • A (Annuity): A series of equal, consecutive, end-of-period payments.
  • i (Interest Rate): The growth rate per period.
  • n (Number of Periods): The duration of the investment.

Interest & Formulas

Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the reward for lending it. We strictly use Compound Interest in GENTECH 2EE3.

Single Payment Compound Interest

F = P(1 + i)^n
P = F(1 + i)^-n

Uniform Series (Annuities)

To find the Present Value of a series of equal payments:

P = A [ ((1 + i)^n - 1) / (i(1 + i)^n) ]

Effective vs. Nominal Rates

If interest is compounded more than once a year (e.g., monthly), the Nominal Rate (r) is not the true cost. You must find the Effective Annual Rate (ia).

ia = (1 + r/m)^m - 1

Arithmetic & Geometric Gradients

Not all cash flows are equal. Some grow or shrink over time.

Arithmetic Gradient (G)

When the cash flow increases by a constant amount (e.g., $100, $200, $300...).

Total P = P(Annuity) + P(Gradient)
P_G = G/i * [ ((1+i)^n - 1)/i - n ] * (1+i)^-n

Geometric Gradient (g)

When the cash flow increases by a constant percentage (e.g., 5% more each year).

If i ≠ g: P = A1 [ (1 - (1+g)^n * (1+i)^-n) / (i - g) ]
If i = g: P = A1 * n / (1 + i)

Present Worth Analysis

Present Worth (PW) or Net Present Value (NPV) reduces all cash flows to a single value at t=0.

The Decision Rule

One Project: If PW ≥ 0, the project is economically viable.

Mutually Exclusive: Choose the project with the highest PW value.

Unequal Lives Problem

You cannot directly compare a 3-year project to a 6-year project using PW. You must use the Least Common Multiple (LCM). In this case, you would assume the 3-year project is repeated twice to reach a 6-year study period.

Annual Worth Analysis

AW converts all cash flows into an equivalent uniform annual series (Annuity) over the life of the project.

The Major Advantage

Unlike PW, the AW of a project is the same whether you look at one life cycle or multiple cycles. You do not need to find the LCM when using AW to compare projects with unequal lives. This makes it the preferred method for most engineering decisions.

Capital Recovery (CR) = P(A/P, i, n) - S(A/F, i, n)
AW = Revenues - Expenses - CR

IRR & Incremental Analysis

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is the interest rate "i" that makes the PW = 0.

The IRR Pitfall

You cannot simply choose the project with the highest IRR. High IRR projects might have very small scales. To compare two projects (A and B), you must:

  • Ensure both projects are viable (Individual IRR > MARR).
  • Calculate the Incremental Cash Flow (B - A), where B is the project with the higher initial investment.
  • Find the IRR of that difference (ΔIRR).
  • If ΔIRR > MARR, choose B. Otherwise, choose A.

CCA & Depreciation

In Canada, we use the Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) for tax purposes. It is a declining balance depreciation method.

Key Rules

  • Half-Year Rule: In the year an asset is acquired, only 50% of its cost is added to the UCC (Undepreciated Capital Cost) for CCA calculation.
  • UCC: The remaining balance of the asset class.
  • Tax Shield: Because CCA is a "paper expense," it reduces taxable income, effectively saving you money (CCA * Tax Rate).
UCC_ending = UCC_beginning + Additions - Disposals - CCA

Replacement Analysis

This is the "Defender vs. Challenger" logic. We ask: "Should we keep what we have, or buy the new version?"

Economic Service Life (ESL)

The ESL is the number of years "n" that minimizes the total Annual Worth of Cost. As years go by, Capital Recovery costs decrease (the initial hit is spread out), but Maintenance/Operating costs increase.

Decision Logic

1. Find the Minimum AW of the Challenger (its EAC*).

2. Find the Marginal Cost of the Defender for one more year.

3. If Marginal Cost (Defender) > Min AW (Challenger), Replace Now.
